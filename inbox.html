Market Overview

The United Kingdom is Europe's largest offshore wind market. In 2025, wind alone met 31% of UK electricity demand — with total wind capacity reaching 32.9 GW (16 GW offshore, 16 GW onshore). The UK connected 1,049 MW of new offshore wind in 2025 across two wind farms — Dogger Bank Phase A (834 MW) and Neart na Gaoithe (216 MW) — plus 201 MW of onshore wind.[1]

In line with the government's Clean Power 2030 agenda, the January 2026 AR7 auction awarded a record 8.4 GW of CfD contracts — 8,245 MW across six bottom-fixed offshore projects (at £91–£109/MWh) and 193 MW of floating wind (at £258/MWh), plus a further 1.3 GW of onshore wind at £72.24/MWh. Contract duration extended from 15 to 20 years from AR7.[2,3]

2026–2030 Outlook (WindEurope): The UK is expected to add 15 GW of offshore and 7 GW of onshore wind between 2026 and 2030, bringing total offshore capacity to ~32 GW. This would fall short of the government's 50 GW offshore target. Curtailment, grid congestion and Scotland–England transmission bottlenecks remain structural constraints.[1]

⚖️ Marginal Cost Pricing (Wholesale)
The wholesale price is set by the most expensive source needed — typically unabated gas. Even zero-marginal-cost renewables receive this price. This is why cheap renewable electricity still drives high wholesale bills, and the core rationale for CfDs as revenue floor instruments.[11,13]
🔄 REMA Reform (2025–2029)
REMA confirmed in 2025 that GB will retain a single national wholesale market — rejecting zonal pricing. Focus shifts to TNUoS reform, connections, and balancing efficiency. Full implementation by 2029.[12,13]

Market Overview

Spain is one of Europe's most dynamic renewable energy markets and the continent's #1 PPA market. Wind met 23% of electricity demand in 2025, with total wind capacity reaching 33.2 GW (all onshore) — the second largest wind fleet in Europe after Germany. Spain added 1.56 GW of new onshore wind in 2025, with an average turbine size of 5.7 MW.[1]

Spain retained its position as Europe's dominant corporate PPA market, with 3.9 GW contracted across 48 deals in 2025. Wind PPA volumes surged 227% year-on-year to 706 MW as corporates sought to diversify their solar-heavy portfolios. Despite this strength, negative power prices were recorded 6.3% of the time in 2025 — up sharply from prior years — compressing solar capture rates to just 51%.[2]

2026–2030 Outlook (WindEurope): Spain is expected to add only 7.5 GW of onshore wind over 2026–2030, reaching ~38 GW by 2030 — well short of the 59 GW NECP target. Grid constraints, failed auction rounds and a lack of auction framework details are the main barriers. Repowering schemes may partly offset the greenfield slowdown.[1]

⚖ MIBEL Wholesale Market

Market Overview

Italy closed 2025 with 83.5 GW of total installed renewable capacity. Wind installed 579 MW of new onshore capacity in 2025, with average turbine size rising to 4.9 MW — though total wind installations fell 15% year-on-year, underscoring persistent permitting and grid bottlenecks. Wind met 8% of Italy's electricity demand, with cumulative capacity at 13.5 GW.[3]

Italy is the #1 EU market for utility-scale BESS. The country deployed ~5 GWh of grid-scale battery storage in 2025, continuing to lead Europe through its MACSE capacity auction scheme. The first MACSE round awarded 1.5 GWh in September 2025. Italy's merchant wholesale price averaged €110/MWh in 2025 — the highest among major EU economies — making it one of Europe's most attractive markets for renewable developers.[1,4]

2026–2030 Outlook (WindEurope): Italy is expected to install 4 GW of onshore wind over 2026–2030, reaching 17 GW total — well below the PNIEC 2030 target of 26 GW. Permitting involving the Soprintendenze cultural heritage authorities and lengthy grid connection queues (350+ GW pending) remain the critical structural barrier. [3,7]

⚖️ Zonal Pricing (from 2025)
Italy is one of Europe's most price-congested markets, with the wholesale market segmented into different price zones up to 27% of the time in 2023 due to grid bottlenecks. From 2025, the single national reference price (PUN) was eliminated as settlement reference — only zonal prices (PZO) are used. This affects PPA structures and merchant revenues significantly, especially for southern assets. [8,10]
☀️ Why Italy's Prices Are High
Italy's average wholesale price of €110/MWh in 2025 was the highest among major EU economies — over 80% above France and 70% above Spain. The gap stems from heavy reliance on imported gas for dispatchable power, structural north–south grid congestion, and limited interconnection capacity. For renewable developers, this premium materially improves merchant and PPA economics. [4,8]

Market Overview

Germany is Europe's largest wind market with 77.7 GW of installed wind capacity — 68 GW onshore and 9.6 GW offshore. In 2025, the country installed a record 5.74 GW of new wind (5.23 GW onshore, 503 MW offshore), accounting for 30% of all new European wind installations. Wind met 28% of Germany's electricity demand — its highest ever share.[1]

Germany's EEG auction reform is delivering transformational results. In 2025, 14.4 GW of onshore wind was awarded across four rounds at clearing prices of €61–70/MWh — up 31% on 2024. The government also permitted a record 20.8 GW of new projects, with average approval timelines falling to 17 months. Repowering contributed 1.5 GW — a record. Battery storage reached 6.6 GWh deployed in 2025, with Germany's merchant BESS market the most active in the EU.[1,4]

2026–2030 Outlook (WindEurope): Germany is expected to install 46 GW of onshore wind and 6.9 GW of offshore wind by 2030 — by far the largest pipeline in Europe. Total wind capacity should reach approximately 108 GW by 2030, versus a government target of 115 GW under EEG 2023.[1]

⚡ EEG Floating Premium
Germany's EEG uses a floating feed-in premium: generators receive the EPEX day-ahead price plus a top-up to reach the auction reference value when spot falls below. When spot rises above reference, no payment is made (no clawback). This differs from Spain's REER and UK's CfD, where clawback applies. Strike prices are NOT inflation-indexed.
🔋 BESS & aFRR Market
Germany's BESS market is Europe's most active on a merchant basis. The aFRR (automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve) capacity market pays storage assets for holding availability — with 2 GW of total procurement capacity. In 2025, aFRR capacity revenues were the dominant BESS revenue stream, with intraday arbitrage spreads rising 6% year-on-year.

Market Overview

Poland is rapidly transforming from a coal-dominated electricity system into one of Europe's most dynamic renewable growth markets. Total wind capacity reached 11.2 GW in 2025, with wind meeting 14% of electricity demand. The country added 800 MW of onshore wind in 2025, constrained by the 10H setback rule — which limits turbine siting to a distance of ten times the tip height from dwellings.[1]

Poland's offshore wind market had its breakthrough year in December 2025, when the country held its first competitive offshore wind CfD auction, awarding 3.4 GW across three Baltic Sea projects (Bałtyk I at 1,560 MW, Baltica 9 at 975 MW, Baltic East at 900 MW) at prices of €113–117/MWh. A further 4 GW CfD round is planned for 2027. Poland also led European offshore investment in 2025 with four of six offshore final investment decisions.[1]

2026–2030 Outlook (WindEurope): Poland expects 3.6 GW of new onshore wind and 4.9 GW of offshore wind between 2026–2030, reaching ~14 GW of total wind capacity. The country entered the European PPA top 10 for the first time in 2025, with corporate offtake accelerating sharply in Q4.[1,2]

10H Setback Rule (Onshore Wind)
High
The 10H rule fixes minimum turbine setbacks at ten times the tip height — effectively restricting wind development in most of Poland. Government tried to introduce a 500m blanket rule but President Nawrocki vetoed it. Municipalities can opt for 700m; government working on 500m amendment.[1]
Grid Infrastructure Lag
High
PSE's north-coast grid reinforcement is critical for integrating the 3.4 GW of offshore wind awarded in 2025. Commissioning of Baltic projects by 2030 depends on transmission infrastructure that does not yet exist. Grid connection delays could push projects to 2031–2032.[1]
Dual BESS Transmission Charges
Medium
Poland applies dual transmission charges to BESS — treating storage as both a consumer and a generator. This structural barrier significantly raises storage project costs vs. EU peers. Reform is under discussion but not yet enacted.[4]
Coal Transition Risk
Medium
Coal still generates ~55% of Poland's electricity. Energy security concerns and social factors (mining employment) complicate the transition. EU ETS carbon price trajectory is the primary driver of the coal-to-gas-to-renewable shift.[5]
Onshore CfD Undersubscription
Medium
Poland's 2025 onshore CfD auction awarded only 83 MW — far below available volume — as developers prefer higher merchant and PPA revenues. Auction design may need reform to attract larger volumes going forward.[1]
